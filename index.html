About Me

A Carbohydrate Chemist
Driven by Catalytic Curiosity

I am a synthetic organic chemist with a deep specialisation in carbohydrate chemistry and biomimetic catalysis. My research sits at a compelling intersection — using the structural complexity of glycals and their rearrangements to access pharmacologically relevant scaffolds, while simultaneously designing metal-ligand systems that replicate the enzymatic elegance of nature.

At Pondicherry University under Prof. J. Kandasamy, I developed an efficient synthetic route to D-Rhamnal from D-Glucal and subsequently exploited it to synthesise novel C-2 aryl glycosides via Pd-catalysed arylation — work now being prepared for peer-reviewed publication. My Ferrier-I rearrangement studies produced 2,3-unsaturated glycosides in yields exceeding 95%, establishing robust methodology across four glycal precursors.

I am currently a Junior Research Fellow (JRF) at NIT Calicut under Dr. M. Sankaralingam, where I am exploring biomimetic analogues of Lytic Polysaccharide Monooxygenases (LPMOs) — copper-dependent enzymes central to sustainable biomass valorisation. I am seeking a European PhD position that will allow me to deepen this work at the frontier of synthetic methodology, green catalysis, and chemical biology.

The Work, in Detail

Each project described as a scientific narrative — the question, my specific contribution, and what was discovered.

Nature-inspired Conversion of Biomass to Biofuel: The LPMO Paradigm
Feb 2026 — Present
🏛 National Institute of Technology Calicut, India
Project Associate (JRF) · Guide: Dr. M. Sankaralingam, Associate Professor, Dept. of Chemistry
Scientific Question
"Can we design synthetic molecular systems and functional nanomaterials that replicate the oxidative C–H activation achieved by copper-dependent LPMOs in nature?"
Lytic Polysaccharide Monooxygenases (LPMOs) are copper metalloenzymes that perform the remarkable feat of oxidatively cleaving recalcitrant crystalline cellulose — a bond inert to most conventional reagents. Their catalytic mechanism, involving Cu(I)/Cu(II) redox cycling and reactive oxygen species, represents a paradigm for sustainable biomass valorisation. My role in this JRF project is twofold: (1) design and synthesise biomimetic ligand scaffolds that support Cu-coordination environments analogous to the LPMO active site, enabling molecular catalytic models; and (2) tune the surface chemistry of nanomaterials and MOFs to replicate the key features of LPMO reactivity in heterogeneous systems.
Currently at an early stage; synthesising first-generation ligand candidates and characterising their Cu complexes by UV-Vis, EPR, and cyclic voltammetry to probe redox behaviour.
Ligand Design Cu Coordination Chemistry Cyclic Voltammetry Nanomaterial Functionalisation MOF Chemistry Biomass Valorisation Biomimetic Catalysis
Strategic Synthesis of D-Rhamnal from D-Glucal & Application to C-2 Aryl Glycosides
Jan – May 2025
🏛 Pondicherry University, India
Master's Thesis · Guide: Prof. Dr. J. Kandasamy, Associate Professor, Dept. of Chemistry
Scientific Question
"How can D-glucal serve as a practical precursor to D-rhamnal, and can this rhamnal derivative be leveraged for novel C-2 arylation chemistry?"
D-Rhamnal — a 6-deoxy-L-mannose derived glycal — is a high-value synthetic building block with established utility in glycoconjugate formation, preparation of pharmaceutical intermediates, and as a chiral scaffold in total synthesis. However, its accessibility from commercial starting materials is limited by lengthy routes. I devised an efficient synthetic strategy starting from D-glucal, involving selective modifications to the C-6 hydroxyl group and controlling the C-5 configuration. The rhamnal product was subsequently employed as a pseudoglycal substrate in a palladium-catalysed C-2 arylation protocol, generating novel C-aryl glycosides under mild conditions with investigation of stereochemical outcomes and protecting group effects across diverse pyranosides.
Successfully synthesised D-rhamnal via an efficient route; Pd-catalysed arylation delivered C-2 aryl glycoside products with promising stereoselectivity — manuscript under preparation.

Ferrier-I Rearrangements of Glycals to 2,3-Unsaturated Glycosides
July – Dec 2024
🏛 Pondicherry University, India
Summer Internship · Guide: Prof. Dr. J. Kandasamy
Scientific Question
"Can we systematically map the Ferrier Type-I rearrangement across structurally diverse glycal precursors to establish a robust, high-yielding methodology?"
The Ferrier rearrangement transforms glycals into 2,3-unsaturated glycosides — versatile intermediates for glycoconjugate synthesis, natural product total synthesis, and drug discovery. Although known, the scope across different glycal architectures (differing in C-6 substitution and absolute configuration) had not been comprehensively benchmarked under uniform conditions. I systematically synthesised 2,3-unsaturated glycosides using four glycal precursors — D-glucal, D-galactal, D-rhamnal, and L-rhamnal — under Ferrier Type-I conditions, optimising the reaction parameters for each substrate and characterising all products by NMR and mass spectrometry. Yields exceeded 95% across all substrates, demonstrating the robustness of the methodology.
Established a reproducible Ferrier-I protocol across four glycal precursors with >95% yield; provided a solid methodological foundation for the thesis-stage C-2 arylation work.

Literature Review — Innovations in the Ferrier Reaction: Green Chemistry & Catalytic Strategies
Jan – May 2025
🏛 Pondicherry University, India
Formal Literature Review · Guide: Prof. Dr. J. Kandasamy
Conducted a systematic analysis of 25+ peer-reviewed publications covering the evolution of the Ferrier rearrangement from classical Lewis acid-mediated conditions to modern green and catalytic approaches. Compared catalysts (BF₃·OEt₂, Amberlyst, zeolites, ionic liquids, photocatalytic systems) across dimensions of yield, selectivity, solvent, temperature, and waste generation. This critical reading not only underpinned my own experimental work but sharpened my ability to evaluate mechanistic proposals and identify genuine methodological innovations versus incremental variations.
Produced a structured comparative analysis; developed critical evaluation skills central to identifying the gap that motivated the C-2 arylation project.

Research Vision

The Problems I Want to Solve

My PhD goal — in the words a professor needs to hear in the first 60 seconds.

"Two of the most pressing challenges in modern synthetic chemistry converge precisely at the boundary of my expertise. First: the carbohydrate chemical space — arguably the most structurally complex and biologically essential class of natural products — remains extraordinarily difficult to access synthetically. Despite a century of glycochemistry, the selective construction of C-glycosidic bonds, particularly with control over anomeric configuration and C-2 functionality, continues to require lengthy routes incompatible with rapid analogue synthesis in drug discovery. My Ferrier-arylation work has given me a direct window into this problem, and I believe new C–H and C–M activation strategies applied to glycal platforms offer genuinely unexplored solutions.

Second: the enzyme family of LPMOs has revealed that nature solves the problem of activating inert C–H bonds in polysaccharides using elegant copper coordination chemistry — a feat synthetic chemists have only begun to replicate. My JRF work at NIT Calicut positions me at this exact frontier. My PhD ambition is to design the next generation of biomimetic Cu catalysts that bridge the mechanistic precision of the LPMO active site with the synthetic accessibility demanded by scalable biomass valorisation."

The Codée group's mechanistic rigour in understanding anomeric selectivity, neighbouring-group participation, and the conformational dynamics of glycosyl donors closely mirrors the stereochemical questions I encountered in my C-2 arylation work. Their computational/experimental approach to glycosylation mechanism is something I want to contribute to and learn from.

Prof. Sels's group works precisely at the interface of catalysis and biomass conversion that defines my JRF project — and their integration of material science, mechanistic chemistry, and sustainability metrics is a model I aspire to follow. A PhD in this group would allow me to extend my LPMO-mimicry work into real heterogeneous systems with direct bio-refinery impact.
